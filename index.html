Abstract

Diffusion based speech enhancement restores plausible clean speech by iteratively applying a learned speech prior to a noisy mixture. However, efficient deployment on humanoid and embodied systems requires a frozen model to operate in real world acoustic scenes whose distributions shift with microphones, rooms, ego noise, and background sources. We formulate noisy speech as a hierarchy of physical structure, where reliable acoustic statistics such as the noise floor and articulation rhythm can be measured from the incoming mixture, whereas ambiguous fine speech detail remains the responsibility of the learned prior. Following this, we propose PACE, a training free physical prior adaptive conditioning module for diffusion and flow based speech enhancement. PACE estimates physical priors from each noisy utterance, forms a corrected mixture, and injects it into frozen samplers through the conditioning signal and starting state, while constrained step optimization selects the smallest validated number of function evaluations (NFE) that preserves quality. Across multiple diffusion based backbones and deployment regimes, PACE improves robustness under noise shift and reduces inference cost when physical priors explain structure that the sampler would otherwise need to generate.

Method

Fewer steps, equal or better quality

Across four frozen backbones (SGMSE+, SB, FlowSE, StoRM) and five benchmarks, every adopted +PACE operating point runs below the backbone default NFE, with speedups from 1.25× to 8.3×, 15 of 20 cells significantly above the backbone grid best and one disclosed loss of 0.02 PESQ.

What the correction does

Listening examples

Fifteen utterances, three per benchmark. Each card plays the noisy input, the frozen backbone at its default NFE, the same backbone with +PACE at a reduced NFE, and the clean reference. Examples are selected where +PACE improves both PESQ against the clean reference and non intrusive perceptual scores (UTMOS, DNSMOS OVRL) over the backbone. Starting a clip pauses the others. Clips are 16 kHz mono.

Live on a humanoid head

Three of the utterances above, played back in a real room and captured by the raw front microphone of an Ameca humanoid head while the head was in motion. The clips document the deployment capture chain, real room acoustics, servo and fan noise of the moving head, with no studio processing. For two utterances the +PACE output on this real channel is included, with reference free DNSMOS overall scores on each clip. Quantitative evaluation on this recording channel is outside the scope of the paper.
